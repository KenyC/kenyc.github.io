Introduction

Ordinary cumulativity

Ordinary cumulative sentences feature a transitive verb and two plural arguments: X V’ed Y.

(1) The cooks opened the oysters.

(In some cases,) we can characterize the truth-condition of (1) in terms of what individual cooks did to individual oysters.

(2) Cumulative truth-conditions1:
Every oyster was opened by a cook.
Every cook opened an oyster.
\(\forall\exists\land\forall\exists\)

\(\rightsquigarrow\) simplifying assumption: no collective action occurs.

Lexical analysis: that’s just what “open” means (e.g. Kratzer (2003))2.

(3) (With simplifying assumption)
\[\textsf{open}(Y)(X) \Leftrightarrow \begin{array}{rl} &\forall x\prec X, \exists y\prec Y, \textsf{open}(y)(x)\\ \land& \forall y\prec Y, \exists x\prec X, \textsf{open}(y)(x) \end{array} \]

The paradox of cumulative readings of (singular) quantifiers

The case of “every”

A simplification of Schein (1993) ’s famous video-game examples:

(4) a. The cooks opened every oyster.
b. Truth-conditions:
Every cook opened an oyster.
Every oyster was opened by a cook.
\(\rightsquigarrow\) the cooks opened the oysters.

If every is a regular universal quantifier, and composition runs as in Heim and Kratzer (1998):

(5) a.
b. The cooks opened every oyster.
c. By compositional rules:
The cooks opened oyster 1
and the cooks opened oyster 2
…
\(\forall x\in \textsf{oyster}, \textsf{open}(x)(\iota\textsf{cooks})\)
d. By cumulative denotation of open:
Every cook opened oyster 1 and oyster 1 was opened by some cook
and every cook opened oyster 2 and oyster 2 was opened by some cook
…
\(\forall x\in \textsf{oyster},\left(\forall y\prec \iota\textsf{cooks}, \textsf{open}(x)(y)\right)\land\left(\exists y\prec \iota\textsf{cooks}, \textsf{open}(x)(y)\right)\)
e. Every cook opened oyster 1
and every cook opened oyster 2
…
\(\forall x\in \textsf{oyster},\forall y\prec \iota\textsf{cooks}, \textsf{open}(x)(y)\)
f. Every cook opened every oyster.

Puzzle I: how can “every” in object position yield a cumulative reading?

More puzzling even, subject/object asymmetries (Kratzer 2003):

(6) a. ? Every cook opened the oysters
b. Truth-conditions:
Every cook opened every oyster

(8) Non-partitive most
a. Most athletes lifted the truck. (Crnič 2010)
b. Most lawyers7 hired a secretary they7 liked. (Kamp and Reyle 2013)

This motivates treating non-partitive most as counting singularities:

(9) \([\![\text{most athletes}]\!](q) =\#\left\lbrace x\in \textsf{athlete} \middle| \textsf{q}(x)\right\rbrace > \frac12 \#\textsf{athlete}\)

There is a cumulative-like reading to most:

(10) a. The cooks opened most oysters.
b. Truth-conditions:
Most oysters were opened by a cook.
Every cook opened an oyster.

If a) open has a cumulative denotation, b) most means the same as it does in (9), then this reading is unexpected:

(11) \(\#\left\lbrace x\in \textsf{oyster} \middle| \textsf{open}(x)(\iota\textsf{cooks})\right\rbrace > \frac12 \#\textsf{oyster}\)
\(\Leftrightarrow\#\left\lbrace x\in \textsf{oyster} \middle| \forall y\prec\iota\textsf{cooks}, \textsf{open}(x)(y)\right\rbrace > \frac12 \#\textsf{oyster}\)
“More than half the oysters were opened by every cook.”
\(\neq\) attested truth-conditions

There are subjet/object asymmetries here as well:

(12) a. ? Most cooks opened the oysters.
b. Truth-conditions:
The proportion of cooks who opened all the oysters was more than half.
c. ? Most US voters will vote for two parties. (Crnič 2010)

The problem of cumulative readings of quantifiers
  • For simple transitive sentences, cumulative truth-conditions can be derived via cumulative denotations for verbs.
  • However, cumulative denotations generate too strong a reading when singular quantifiers like every are involved.
  • The problem is quite general and extends to singular quantifiers of various sorts

This paradox has elicited a diverse range of reactions:

  • evidence for the necessity of events and Neo-Davidsonian separation:
    Schein (1993), Kratzer (2003), …
  • cumulative composition and cross-categorial pluralities:
    Haslinger and Schmitt (2018), Haslinger and Schmitt (2020), …
  • “every NP” is or can be a referential expression:
    Champollion (2010), Landman (2000), …

Claim: as far as these data points are concerned, there is no need for enriched composition.

Cumulativity from the perspective of homogeneity

Observation: if negative sentences were all that we had to account for, solving the problem of cumulative readings of quantifiers would be extremely simple.

The peculiar truth-conditions of negative sentences

The truth-conditions of negative cumulative sentences sentences are not what we expect: the truth-conditions of (23) are not simply the complement of the cumulative truth-conditions.

(23) The cooks didn’t open the oysters.
a. Attested truth-conditions: \(\neg\exists\exists\)
No cook opened any oyster.
b. Expected truth-conditions: \(\neg\forall\exists\vee\neg\forall\exists\)
Not every cook opened an oyster or not every oyster was opened by a cook.

There is homogeneity. Homogeneity is also attested in intransitive sentences.

(24) Joana and Marius didn’t smile.
a. Attested truth-conditions: \(\neg\exists\)
Neither Joana nor Marius smiled
b. Expected truth-conditions: \(\neg\forall\)
They didn’t both smile.

There is also homogeneity in cumulative readings with every:

(25) The cooks didn’t open every oyster.
a. Attested truth-conditions: \(\neg\forall\exists\)
Not every oyster was opened by a cook.
b. Expected truth-conditions: \(\neg\forall\exists\vee\neg\forall\exists\)
Not every oyster was opened by a cook or not every cook opened an oyster.

A theory of negative sentences

Why is this observation important? We now have conflicting evidence regarding the meaning of open

(26) a. The cooks opened the oysters.
b. The cooks didn’t open the oysters.

Had we started with negative sentences, we would have written the denotation of open differently.

(27) a. The cooks didn’t open the oysters.
b. \(\neg\textsf{opened}(\iota\textsf{cooks})(\iota\textsf{oysters})\) (= expected truth-conditions)
\(\Leftrightarrow\)
\(\neg\exists x\prec \iota\textsf{cooks}, \exists y \prec \iota\textsf{oysters}, \textsf{opened}(y)(x)\) (= attested truth-conditions)

Call the meaning in (28 a) is the existential denotation for open.

(28) Weak and strong meaning for open
a. Existential denotation for open
\(\textsf{open}(Y)(X) \Leftrightarrow \exists x\prec X, \exists y \prec Y, \textsf{open}(y)(x)\)
b. Cumulative denotation for open
\(\textsf{open}(Y)(X) \Leftrightarrow \begin{array}{rl} &\forall x\prec X, \exists y\prec Y, \textsf{open}(y)(x)\\ \land& \forall y\prec Y, \exists x\prec X, \textsf{open}(y)(x) \end{array}\)

Unlike the positive case, the existential denotation works in both negative sentences with and without every.

(29) a. The cooks didn’t open every oyster.
b.
c. \(\neg\forall y\in \textsf{oyster},\ \textsf{opened}(y)(\iota\textsf{cooks})\)
\(\Leftrightarrow \neg\forall y \in \textsf{oyster}, \exists x\prec \iota\textsf{cooks}, \textsf{opened}(y)(x)\)
\(=\) attested truth-conditions

The success of the existential denotation cannot be understated: it covers all other cases considered and more.

In positive sentences, adopting the existential denotation misses some inferences of positive sentences (represented in gray).

Proposal: derive these inferences as implicatures.

Bar-Lev’s account of homogeneity.

A similar problem arise outside of cumulativity: to account for the truth-contions of (36 a), we may want to posit the existential denotation in (36 b).

(36) a. The children didn’t smile.
\(\rightsquigarrow\) no child smiled.
b. \(\textsf{smiled}(X) \Leftrightarrow \exists x\prec X, \textsf{smiled}(x)\)
(37) a. The children smiled.
b. Truth-conditions:4 Some child smiled.
Every child smiled.

To account for the presense of the gap in the case of (37), Moshe E. Bar-Lev (2018) proposes that a mechanism akin to implicatures, Free Choice implicatures specifically.

Rationale:

  • implicatures are polarity-sensitive ; so are these inferences.
  • implicatures are context-sensitive ; so are these inferences (Malamud 2012)
  • children don’t derive (certain) implicatures at the same rate that adults do ; same for the inference in (37 b) (Tieu et al. 2015)

Yardsticks: analogies with known implicatures

Analogy with Free Choice implicatures. (Moshe E. Bar-Lev 2018)

If (38) is a case of implicature, it is a case of an underlying existential (\(\exists\)) being strengthened to a universal (\(\forall\)):

(38) a. The children slept.
b. Truth-conditions:
Some child slept.
Every child slept.
(39) a. The children didn’t sleep.
b. Truth-conditions:
No child slept.

In FC implicatures, an underlyingly disjunctive meaning (\(\vee\)) is strengthened in positive sentences to a conjunctive meaning (\(\land\)).

(40) a. You can have ice-cream or cake.
b. Truth-conditions:
You either can have ice-cream or can have cake.
You can have ice-cream.
You can have cake.
(41) a. You can’t have ice-cream or cake.
b. Truth-conditions:
You can neither have ice-cream or cake.

Analogy with distributive implicatures. The analogy with FC implicatures is not very apt for cumulative sentences. The desired strengthening is not simply converting an existential to a universal.

(42) a. The cooks opened every oyster.
b. Truth-conditions:
Every oyster was opened by some cook.
Every cook opened an oyster.

But it does resemble another implicature process: distributive implicatures.

(43) a. Every ambassador speaks Mandarin or Tagalog.
b. Truth-conditions:
Every ambassador speaks Mandarin or Tagalog. (\(\forall \gg \vee\))
Some ambassador speaks Mandarin.
Some ambassador speaks Tagalog.

Generalizing \(\vee\) to \(\exists\), this is precisely the type of strengthening observed in positive sentences.

(44) a. The cooks opened every oyster.
b. Truth-conditions:
Every oyster was opened by Joana or Marius or Barnaby or … (\(\forall \gg \exists\))
Joana opened some oyster.
Marius opened some oyster.
Barnaby opened some oyster.
…

Proposal: cumulativity from homogeneity

  1. Transitive verbs do not have cumulative denotations. They underlyingly have existential denotations. (cf Bayer (2013), Landman (1989))

    \[\textsf{open}(Y)(X) \Leftrightarrow \exists x\prec X, \exists y \prec Y, \textsf{open}(y)(x)\]

  2. In positive sentences, exhaustive participation implicatures arise. They arise through a process of recursive exhaustification.

exh exh The cooks opened every oyster.

Recursive exhaustification

Canonical implicatures:

(47) a. Some people at my office are nice.
b. Truth-conditions:
Some people at my office are nice.
Not all people at my office are nice

In the grammatical tradition (Chierchia, Fox, and Spector 2012), these implicatures are derived by application of a covert operator exh, whose meaning is akin to only:

(48) exh some people at my office are nice.
Prejacent: some people at my office are nice.
Alternatives:
\(\circ\) all of them are
Strengthened meaning:
Some people at my office are nice
but not all are.

These inferences we are interested in are different: they are not simply the negation of an alternative to the sentence

(49) a. You can have ice cream or cake.
\(\rightsquigarrow\) you can have both
b. Everyone here speaks Tagalog or Mandarin. (Crnič, Chemla, and Fox 2015) \(\rightsquigarrow\) some speak Tagalog ; some speak Mandarin

FC implicatures (Fox 2007) and distributive implicatures (Moshe E. Bar-Lev and Fox 2016) are derived via recursive exhaustification.

(50) exh exh You can have ice-cream or cake.
Prejacent: exh You can have ice-cream or cake.
Alternatives:
\(\circ\) exh You can have ice-cream.
\(\circ\) exh You can have cake.
Strengthened meaning:
You either can have ice-cream or can have cake.
and it’s not the case that you can only have ice-cream.
and it’s not the case that you can only have cake.
\(\approx\) you can have both
(51) exh exh Every ambassador speaks Mandarin or Tagalog.
Prejacent: exh Every ambassador speaks Mandarin or Tagalog.
Alternatives:
\(\circ\) exh every ambassador speaks Mandarin.
\(\approx\) every ambassador speaks Mandarin and no ambassador speaks Tagalog.
\(\circ\) exh every ambassador speaks Tagalog.
\(\approx\) every ambassador speaks Tagalog and no ambassador speaks Mandarin.
Strengthened meaning:
Every ambassador speaks Mandarin or Tagalog
and it’s not the case that every ambassador speaks Mandarin and no ambassador speaks Tagalog.
and it’s not the case that every ambassador speaks Tagalog and no ambassador speaks Mandarin.
\(\rightsquigarrow\) some ambassador speaks Mandarin ; some ambassador speaks Tagalog.

(58) a. Every cook opened the oysters
b. exh exh every cook exh exh opened the oysters.
\(\Rightarrow\) every cook opened every oyster

b. exh exh the cooks exh exh opened every oyster.
\(\Rightarrow\) every cook opened every oyster

What drives the difference? The low scope of the existential meaning associated with “the cooks”.

Conclusion

The theory

Proposal: cumulativity from homogeneity

  1. Transitive verbs do not have cumulative denotations. They underlyingly have existential denotations. (cf Bayer (2013), Landman (1989))

    \[\textsf{open}(Y)(X) \Leftrightarrow \exists x\prec X, \exists y \prec Y, \textsf{open}(y)(x)\]

  2. In positive sentences, exhaustive participation implicatures arise. They arise through a process of recursive exhaustification.

exh exh The cooks opened every oyster.

The implications

  • No need for new rules of compositions or new meanings for every (Schein 1993), but need for “fancy implicatures” (independently attested)
  • Show the importance of negative sentences in understanding the problem of cumulativity.
  • A general approach to cumulative readings of quantification.
